# Source anchors from *Magnifica Humanitas* (Leo XIV, 15 May 2026)

Key passages underpinning each tension. Read when a deeper justification would help, or when the user pushes back on a placement.

## On the Babel/Pentecost frame itself

- **§7 (Babel as project):** "a single language, a single technology, a single direction... a project conceived without reference to God, supported by a uniformity that eliminated diversity and that chose homogenization over communion."
- **§8 (Nehemiah/Jerusalem):** "shared responsibility of all... an undertaking with God at the center, which rebuilds relationships before rebuilding with stones."
- **§9 (technology never neutral):** "technology is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate and use it."
- **§10 (the Babel syndrome to avoid):** "the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language — even a digital one — can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance."

## On concentration of power

- **§5:** "the main drivers of development are private, often transnational, parties that are endowed with resources and the capacity to intervene that surpass those of many Governments. Technological power thus takes on an unprecedented, predominantly 'private' aspect."
- **§67:** "among the goods that are universally intended for everyone, we must also include new forms of property, such as patents, algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure and data."
- **§71:** "the highest level is not the State, but rather major economic and technological actors that exercise *de facto* power over the conditions of everyday life."

## On uniformity vs. plurality

- **§10:** "the plurality of voices and visions... the possibility of building together, of transforming diversity into a resource and of making listening and dialogue the common ground."
- **§26 (polyhedron):** "the one truth of the Gospel is reflected from different angles."

## On replacement vs. augmentation

- **§12:** "All too often, we place our hope in unlimited 'upgrades,' in forms of progress that exacerbate inequalities, and in immediate solutions incapable of healing people's wounds."
- **§37:** Work is "a fundamental good for the person, a principle of economic activity and the key to the entire societal question."
- **§94 (Paul VI quoted):** "the most extraordinary scientific progress... unless accompanied by authentic moral and social progress, will in the long run go against man" — and Leo's gloss: "an increase in means without a growth in humanity: 'having more' without 'being more.'"

## On transparency

- **§71:** "transparency regarding algorithms, equitable access to data and avenues for recourse."
- **§86 (the Church's own examen):** "a culture of transparency, accountability and evaluation as key practices for missionary transformation." (Applied internally — but the principle generalizes.)

## On the technocratic paradigm (efficiency-only)

- **§92:** "the tendency to let the logic of efficiency, control and profit alone shape personal, social and economic decisions... it begins to dictate what matters and what can be discarded, reducing creation to an object of exploitation and human beings to mere cogs in a system."
- **§93:** "More power does not necessarily imply something better."
- **§43 (quoting *Laudato Si'*):** "the cry of the earth and the cry of the poor cannot be separated."

## On the rejected stones / preferential option for the poor

- **§16:** "the 'rejected stones' — the poor, the sick, the migrants and the least among us — will become the cornerstone."
- **§78:** "social justice begins with the least among us... a preferential option for the poor."
- **§80:** "individuals and peoples hindered or denied access to basic technologies, communities exposed to invasive surveillance and social groups penalized by opaque algorithms that perpetuate prejudice and discrimination."

## On the encyclical's own register

- **§14:** "We cannot condone naïve enthusiasms, nor fuel unfounded fears. Instead, let us establish standards for discernment — the dignity of the human person, the universal destination of goods, the preferential option for the poor, care for our common home and peace — and let us translate these standards into practices."
